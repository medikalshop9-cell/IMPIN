1. EXECUTIVE SUMMARY

Inflation measurement in sub-Saharan Africa and North Africa suffers from a
fundamental data lag: official Consumer Price Index (CPI) releases are
published weeks or months after the period they measure, by which point
economic damage from supply shocks or currency pressures is already
entrenched.

This project builds an Informal Market Price Intelligence Network (IMPIN) —
a pipeline that scrapes and aggregates commodity prices from digital informal
market proxies (e-commerce platforms, social media, and trader networks),
constructs a real-time price index, and applies machine learning to detect
inflation signals before official CPI releases.

The core academic contribution is a Granger causality demonstration: that
informal digital price data leads official CPI releases by 2–4 weeks, making
IMPIN a viable nowcasting instrument for policymakers, central banks, and
investors operating in data-sparse environments. The pilot focuses on Accra,
Ghana, with an extensible framework applicable to Lagos and Casablanca.


2. PROBLEM STATEMENT

2.1  The African Data Gap

Traditional macroeconomic models assume timely, granular, and reliable price
data. This assumption collapses in the context of African economies, where:

  - Official CPI releases lag by 4–8 weeks on average.
  - Informal sectors account for approximately 55% of Sub-Saharan Africa's
    GDP and 80% of its labour force, yet remain systematically under-measured
    (African Development Bank, 2019).
  - Survey-based data collection is expensive, infrequent, and geographically
    sparse.
  - Central banks and investors operate with significantly delayed signals.

2.2  The Opportunity

Digital informal market activity — mobile commerce listings, trader price
posts on WhatsApp and Facebook, and e-commerce platform data — generates a
continuous, high-frequency stream of price information. This data, previously
unstructured and unexploited, can be harvested, structured, and transformed
into a leading economic indicator.

2.3  Research Gap

While developed-market nowcasting (e.g., Google Trends-based inflation
forecasting) is well-studied, African informal market price intelligence
remains largely unexplored. This project targets that gap with a methodology
purpose-built for low-resource, high-informality contexts.


3. RESEARCH QUESTIONS

  RQ1: Can scraped informal market price data be systematically aggregated
       into a reliable weekly price index for Accra, Ghana?

  RQ2: Does the IMPIN index Granger-cause official CPI releases, and if so,
       by how many weeks?

  RQ3: Can ML models (LSTM, Prophet) produce statistically significant
       inflation forecasts using IMPIN data as input?

  RQ4: Can anomaly detection identify commodity price shocks before they are
       reflected in official statistics?


4. OBJECTIVES

4.1  Primary Objectives

  - Build an automated scraping pipeline collecting daily commodity prices
    from Jumia Ghana, Tonaton, and social media proxies.
  - Construct a weekly Informal Market Price Index (IMPI) mirroring Ghana's
    CPI basket composition.
  - Demonstrate Granger causality between IMPI and Ghana Statistical Service
    CPI releases.
  - Deploy a forecasting model (LSTM or Prophet) achieving lower RMSE than a
    naive baseline.

4.2  Secondary Objectives

  - Build a real-time Streamlit dashboard visualising price trends and anomaly
    alerts.
  - Establish a replicable framework extensible to Lagos (Nigeria) and
    Casablanca (Morocco).
  - Produce a policy brief targeting central bank and development finance
    audiences.

6. METHODOLOGY

6.1  Phase 1 — Data Collection Pipeline

A modular Python scraper is built for each source using BeautifulSoup and
Playwright (for JavaScript-rendered pages). Commodity targets mirror Ghana's
CPI basket:

  CPI Category                   Tracked Commodities
  ─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
  Food & Non-Alcoholic Beverages  Rice, maize, tomatoes, onions, cooking
                                  oil, chicken, fish, eggs, bread, sugar.

  Transport Proxies               Petrol/diesel retail prices scraped from
                                  GOIL and Total Ghana listings.

  Housing Proxies                 LPG gas cylinder prices, kerosene.

  Personal Care                   Soap, detergent — sampled on Jumia.

6.2  Phase 2 — Price Index Construction

Weekly price averages per commodity are computed and weighted using Ghana GSS
CPI basket weights. The resulting Informal Market Price Index (IMPI) is
normalised to a base period of January 2026 = 100.

An NLP layer using spaCy and regex extracts price mentions from unstructured
text posts (e.g., "Tomatoes GH₵12/basket today, Makola market"). Named entity
recognition identifies commodity names, units, and prices.

6.3  Phase 3 — Granger Causality Analysis

The core statistical test is the Granger causality test between the weekly
IMPI series and official monthly CPI releases. If IMPI Granger-causes CPI at
lag k, the hypothesis holds: informal prices predict official inflation k
periods ahead.

Vector Autoregression (VAR) models will be used for multivariate causality
testing, incorporating exchange rate and global commodity price controls.

6.4  Phase 4 — Forecasting Models

  Model         Role & Rationale
  ─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
  Prophet       Baseline time-series decomposition — handles seasonality,
  (Meta)        trend breaks, and missing data gracefully.

  LSTM          Deep learning sequence model — captures non-linear temporal
  (PyTorch)     patterns in price dynamics.

  ARIMAX        Classical econometric benchmark — uses IMPI as an exogenous
                regressor for CPI forecasting.

  Ensemble      Weighted average of Prophet + LSTM outputs, optimised on
                the validation set.

6.5  Phase 5 — Anomaly Detection

Isolation Forest and Z-score thresholding are applied to the weekly IMPI
series to flag commodity price spikes. Alerts are surfaced in the dashboard
with a confidence score and affected commodity list. Alerts are compared
ex-post to inflation reports to measure precision and recall.

6.6  Phase 6 — Dashboard

A Streamlit application presents:
  (1) A choropleth map of price levels by district.
  (2) A time-series chart comparing IMPI vs. official CPI.
  (3) An anomaly alert feed.
  (4) A 4-week inflation forecast with confidence intervals.

9. EXPECTED OUTCOMES

9.1  Academic Contributions

  - A peer-review-ready paper demonstrating that informal digital price data
    Granger-causes official CPI in Ghana.
  - A reproducible methodology for informal price nowcasting applicable to
    other data-sparse African economies.
  - Benchmarked ML model performance (RMSE, MAE, directional accuracy) across
    Prophet, LSTM, and ARIMAX.

9.2  Practical Deliverables

  - Open-source scraping and indexing pipeline (GitHub repository).
  - Live Streamlit dashboard with 4-week inflation forecast and anomaly alerts.
  - A policy brief targeting Bank of Ghana and development finance
    institutions.
  - A replication kit (documented code + synthetic dataset) for extending to
    Lagos and Casablanca.

9.3  Success Metrics

  Metric                 Target
  ─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
  Granger Causality      IMPI Granger-causes official CPI at p < 0.05 for
                         at least one lag.

  Forecast Accuracy      LSTM or Prophet achieves lower RMSE than a naive
                         random walk baseline.

  Anomaly Detection      Precision > 70% on known price shock events in
                         the validation period.

  Data Coverage          Minimum 8 weeks of daily price observations across
                         10+ commodities.

  Dashboard              Functional Streamlit app with live data updates
                         and forecast visualisation.

11. LITERATURE ANCHORS

All references below are verified peer-reviewed publications or institutional
reports:

  Cavallo, A. (2018). Scraped Data and Sticky Prices. Review of Economics
  and Statistics, 100(1), 105–119.
  — Foundational methodology for online price scraping and CPI measurement
    bias correction.

  Cavallo, A. & Rigobon, R. (2016). The Billion Prices Project: Using Online
  Prices for Inflation Measurement and Research. Journal of Economic
  Perspectives, 30(2), 151–178.
  — Benchmark framework for multi-country online price index construction
    using scraped data.

  Nakamura, E. & Steinsson, J. (2008). Five Facts About Prices: A
  Reevaluation of Menu Cost Models. Quarterly Journal of Economics, 123(4),
  1415–1464.
  — Empirical foundation for CPI basket composition and price adjustment
    frequency analysis.

  World Food Programme VAM (2023). Food Security and Market Monitoring in
  West Africa. WFP Vulnerability Analysis and Mapping Unit.
  — Primary institutional data source and methodology reference for African
    food market price tracking.

  IMF African Department (2022). Overcoming Data Sparsity: A Machine Learning
  Approach to Track Economic Activity in Sub-Saharan Africa. IMF Working
  Paper WP/22/88.
  — Establishes ML nowcasting as a viable framework for data-sparse African
    environments; directly methodologically analogous to this project.
